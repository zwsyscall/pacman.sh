# history
```bash
for i in $(seq $(history 1 | awk '{print $1}') -1 2013); do history -d $i; done
```

---

It's 2012, and you're outside with your friends. It's warm but not too warm, and it's the last night of summer break. You dread going back to school tomorrow and already can't wait for the next summer vacation. School sucks!

Little do you know, this will be the last summer night with your friends without an omnipresent being peering in on what you're doing, no Big Brother always watching, making sure to document your amazing and, more importantly, embarrassing moments.

What is this ever-watching, elusive Big Brother? That third party ever-present in every interaction thereon after is social media and cameras.  
What was the first platform that affected behavior in a significant manner? You'll instinctively think of the specific app that was popular during your youth. Maybe it was MySpace, maybe Facebook, maybe Snapchat. Everyone thinks of that one dominant platform. Regardless of which one you're envisioning, they all thrive on the same thing: moments saved, shared, and a feigned notion of being "social" when you post there.

As social media usage became normalized, people started recording *everything*. No moment went unrecorded; every victory and every failure was forever saved and shared. After all, what if you wanted to look back on those times? How many of those pictures did you actually look back on? Saving a window into another era is a noble idea, but human memory is finicky. We romanticize events, forget details, and invent new ones as we recall them time and time again. It's the life cycle of a memory. Real life isn't perfect, and neither are our minds. Memories changing with time is part of their nature; we find new meaning in old moments, allowing them to keep living in one form or another.

"What harm is there in wanting to take a snapshot of an event you experienced?" I hear you ask. *None*. Despite my ramblings, I think wanting a snapshot of important events is not only human, it's beautiful. I do it myself. I've got thousands of pictures of my cat and hundreds of photos from every trip I've taken. It makes me feel good to look at them, but I often wish the pictures weren't so perfect. I wish they reflected reality a bit more. That one picture of the sunset I spent 20 minutes trying to capture wasn't worth it. If I could go back, delete the photo, and simply enjoy 20 more minutes on that beautiful beach, I would. 

The thing to keep in mind is that when you take a picture, you consciously step outside of whatever it is you want to immortalize. You are looking through a window: your phone screen. If you record a friend dancing, did you actually see them with your own eyes, or did you see a lower-quality version through the eye of a camera? Can you remember what was happening around your friend, or were you too focused on keeping the lens tracked on them?

You are directly experiencing *less* of the moment you're trying to save. In the worst-case scenario, you spend the entire time trying to record it, meaning the moment is lost forever. While you might have a great video, *you* were never truly there; *you* never experienced it. Would you really rather have a digital asset than a living memory? If the point is to remember, shouldn't you actually live it first? A camera's memory won't do you much good, it's only a narrow snapshot, not a true experience.

Let's get back on topic. Why do I bring up how taking pictures diminishes our experiences? Because a huge number of photos taken today aren't for the sake of remembering; they're taken to *share*. The primary benefit of capturing a moment to keep it forever isn't even part of the equation anymore. You're taking a picture to show other people what you've done, missing out on the experience just to prove to others that you had it. Look around you: how much everyday behavior is driven entirely by social media? How many people choose a cafe simply because they serve food that looks good on camera? Contrary to the old saying, we aren't *actually* eating with our eyes.

That following summer was different, wasn't it? Behavior was directly driven by a need for content. Maybe your friend did a cool trick, but because you didn't get it on camera, you asked them to do it again. It's not just a friend group hanging out anymore; it's you, your friends, and an invisible third party. You want to make sure that third party is having a good time, much to the detriment of your own present moment. In a sense, you live through your phone's camera, you experience events for the sake of sharing them with others instead of actually experiencing them.
