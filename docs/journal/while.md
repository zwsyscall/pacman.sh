This is what my life feels like right now. Arguably not a great thing, but what can I do? I live in these 8-hour cycles, of which the free-time one is the worst because I've got nothing I really want to do. I churn out my walks during the workday, so I can't exactly work out during my free time. I don't really want to go out and see friends because I keep zoning out and I'm bad company (right now). On the flip side, I also don't want to self-isolate because that'd probably lead to worse outcomes. Poetic.

Like I mentioned in my previous post [dmesg](#journal-dmesg), I feel deeply lost. I'm not sure about a lot of things, including the question at my very core of who I am and what I want. Ripping out some of my core beliefs and throwing myself into something that didn't end up working out sure did a number on my previously coherent worldview and set of goals, go figure!

I don't regret it. I really don't. I probably ought not live my life the way society or other people dictate; I should live it in a way that I'm most comfortable with. For as long as I can remember, I've been shackled by society's expectations and norms, even if I was "an outsider." <sub><sub> Editor's note: I mean nerd, so not really an outsider. </sub></sub> For the most part, this is no longer the case. Sure, there's still about 10-30% that I can tell holds me back from doing what I really want. I can feel the (nonexistent) ![slavic cctv](images/slavic-cctv.webp) burning a hole in the back of my neck, and I act appropriately. 

Now I am mostly free, but at what cost? Knowing where I'd roughly end up brought peace to my world; it brought serenity when I most needed it. If a day, a week, or a month was bad, I'd eventually land on my feet and I always knew what to work towards. Every backup plan had a backup, so I always knew what I should do even if all else failed. I don't feel this way anymore, and it's partly due to me explicitly getting rid of "the plan," and partly because of the world changing. This doesn't all go back to that one thing, but it plays a large role in my "letting go" of society's shackles, as I've so earnestly taken to calling them.

Everybody wants to be different within the range of acceptable behaviour; this is the norm. Nobody wants to be too weird; they want to be weird within the confines of what's accepted behaviour. What is acceptable ranges from culture to culture, person to person. There's some kind of global culture that spawned off the back of globalization and the homogenization of our cultures; I think this is probably where I land. I don't feel like a "Finnish man" despite literally being a Finnish man! Culturally I feel more at ease with \<one\> of these funky internet spaces I inhabit.

I think my internal range of acceptable behaviour was just much narrower than it is now; if I were truly ![off the grid, man](images/far-out-man.gif), I'd probably be climbing the walls compared to how I am now. Alas, I don't want to be *too* weird; I'm only human and participate in society. I've still got some sense of acceptable societal norms, they're just broader than they were before. I'm still trying to figure out and piece together my identity, coming to terms with my own external and internal representation of myself. These are big tasks which take a lot of thinking, time, and, eventually, sacrifice.

Side note on the homogeneity of our cultures: I ask you, fellow non-Americans, how many of the big cultural trends in the last 10 years spawned from your country, and how many were downstreamed from America, or whatever other big nation that doesn't even consider your existence? I can think of quite a few foreign trends that popped up here. Even as Finland desperately tries to cling to its culture, the language slowly dies in favour of English and our countryside culture dies out in favour of urbanism. What is it to be Finnish in the current day? What is our culture if not being shy to the point of anti-social behavour, using the shared communal sauna in your block of flats once a week and eating Swedish dishes. I'm not above this; I did my high school education in the IB, and as such, a lot of my teenage years were dominated by English and other cultures. Most of my friends are foreigners and I visit them abroad yearly. My ties here are my folks and a handful of friends I want to keep in touch with. My Finnish skills have definitely eroded from what they would have been had I done a normal high school, but I'm happy with my choice. It feels meaningless to think about "what ifs" with such huge topics as where I studied, there are just too many variables. <sub><sub>Editor's note: I'll probably write a longer piece on culture because it's interesting, and after someone brought it up to me, I've been thinking about it a lot more. </sub></sub>
